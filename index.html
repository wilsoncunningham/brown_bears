About Brown Bears

The brown bear (Ursus arctos) is native to parts of northern Eurasia and North America. Its conservation status is currently Least Concern.

There are many subspecies within the brown bear species, including the Atlas bear and the Himalayan brown bear.

The brown bear (Ursus arctos) is a large mammal native to parts of northern Eurasia and North America. Known for its impressive size and strength, this species has adapted to a variety of habitats, from forests to mountainous regions. Currently, the brown bear's conservation status is Least Concern, indicating a stable population in many of its native areas. There are numerous subspecies within the brown bear species, each adapted to their specific environments. Notable subspecies include the Atlas bear, which was once found in North Africa but is now extinct, and the Himalayan brown bear, which inhabits the mountainous regions of the Himalayas. These diverse subspecies showcase the brown bear's remarkable adaptability and resilience across different ecosystems.

Features

Brown bears are not always completely brown. Some can be reddish or yellowish. They have very large, curved claws and huge paws. Male brown bears are often 30% larger than female brown bears. They can range from 5 feet to 9 feet from head to toe.

Countries with Small Brown Bear Populations

Some countries with smaller brown bear populations include Armenia, Belarus, Bulgaria, China, Finland, France, Greece, India, Japan, Nepal, Poland, Romania, Slovenia, Turkmenistan, and Uzbekistan.
